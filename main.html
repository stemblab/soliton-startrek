Star Trek's solitons are real

[http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geordi_La_Forge Geordi La Forge] was right: [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soliton solitons] *are* exciting---no matter [http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2BQT_iXVL28 what his colleagues may think]. A soliton is a pulse that experiences both nonlinearity and [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dispersion_(water_waves) dispersion]. Nonlinearity sharpens the pulse---taller parts travel faster than shorter parts (like a wave breaking on a beach). Dispersion spreads it out---different frequencies travel at different speeds. With a soliton the sharpening and spreading balance *exactly* (and the balance is stable!). Not that long ago the very idea that such order could exist in a nonlinear system was [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi-Pasta-Ulam_problem new and unexpected]. And the soliton has a further surprise. When two (or more) solitons collide they interact nonlinearly, but eventually emerge unchanged. They appear to behave much like a particle (prot*on*, neutr*on* etc.).
Solitons are a genuine wonder of the nonlinear world. Below you can experiment with solitons of the [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korteweg-de_Vries_equation KdV] equation. Click in the box below to initialize the solitons and run the simulation. Tall solitons experience more nonlinearity and so are faster and narrower (for higher dispersion). Two solitons collide in different ways according to the ratio of their amplitudes. While we use numerical methods here, true solutions are available via the [http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_scattering_transform Inverse Scattering Transform]. The IST is a *nonlinear analog of the Fourier transform*, and one of the great advances of modern mathematical physics. Geordi was right: solitons are exciting---and perhaps just a little weird as well.
